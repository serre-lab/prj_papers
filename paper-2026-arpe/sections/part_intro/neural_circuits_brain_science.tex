\paragraph{The Visual System.} The visual system is a prime example of a complex neural circuit that can be effectively modeled using the formalism introduced in \cref{sec:neural_circuits}. The visual system consists of multiple interconnected brain regions, including the retina, lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN), primary visual cortex (V1), and higher-order visual areas. Each of these regions can be represented as a set of neurons (nodes) with specific connectivity patterns (edges) that define the flow of visual information through the system. \paragraph{Delays and feedbacks.} However, this formalism is not without limitations. The biology of neural circuits includes several more layers of complexity that are not captured by this simple framework. For instance, synaptic plasticity mechanisms allow neural circuits to adapt and learn from experience. Specific cell types, physiological properties such as axon myelination which affects signal propagation speed, along with neuromodulators and countless other biological factors are not explicitly modeled here. We briefly touch on one such aspect which will be of particular interest for \cref{part:convssm}\greg{check that crefs to parts is not broken}, which boils down to time delays in signal propagation.
To model delays in our formalism, each edge $(u, v) \in E$ can be associated with a delay parameter $\tau_{uv} \geq 0$, representing the time it takes for a signal to propagate from neuron $u$ to neuron $v$. The neuron-wise state update equation then becomes:

\begin{equation}
    h_v(t) = F_v\left( \{ h_u(t - \tau_{uv}) : (u, v) \in E \} \right)
\end{equation}

Several factors influence these delays, including axon length and diameter, myelination, and synaptic transmission times. Understanding and modeling these delays is essential for accurately capturing the dynamics of neural circuits and their functional roles in information processing. An example of crucial functionality enabled by delays is the introduction of oscillatory dynamics in recurrent circuits, which are thought to play a role in various cognitive functions such as attention, binding and working memory. Another setting where delays play a crucial role is examplified in the visual system. For instance, magnocellular pathways are known for their rapid signal transmission, which is crucial for processing motion and temporal changes, even at the cost of coarse spatial resolution. In contrast, parvocellular pathways, which process fine spatial details and color, exhibit longer delays. Feedforwards account for slower parvocellular pathways, while feedback connections typically follow rapid magnocellular pathways, allowing for quick top-down modulation of sensory processing. This speed in feedback pathways alows for rapid contextual integration across a wide spatial area, while lateral connections within an area tend to be slower and more spatially constrained.
